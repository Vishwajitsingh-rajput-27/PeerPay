5. Topic of the Activity

"Understanding Ancient Indian Rock-Cut Architecture, Buddhist Monastic Traditions, and Indigenous Engineering Techniques at Bedse Caves"

The activity focused on experiential learning about India's ancient knowledge systems, particularly Buddhist philosophy, architectural excellence, water management systems, and the socio-cultural fabric of ancient Indian society as evidenced through the Bedse cave complex.

6. Objective of the Activity

  • To provide practical exposure to ancient Indian rock-cut architecture and Buddhist monastic traditions
  • To study indigenous water harvesting techniques and sustainable practices
  • To develop observational, analytical and critical thinking skills
  • To foster cultural awareness and understand interdisciplinary connections between history, architecture, and management

Pre-Visit Preparation

Students were given comprehensive orientation about Bedse Caves' historical significance. Faculty conducted preparatory sessions covering Buddhist architecture basics and the Hinayana period. Students were divided into groups of 6-7 members with specific observation tasks.

Cave Architecture Exploration

The Chaitya Hall: Students explored the magnificent 12-meter prayer hall featuring a horseshoe-shaped entrance, monolithic stupa, and rows of pillars with pot-and-foliage capitals showing Hellenistic influence. The guide explained the ingenious natural lighting system and astronomical alignment.

The Vihara: The monastery consists of a central hall with small cells for individual monks. Stone beds and niches for oil lamps demonstrated simple, austere living embodying Buddhist values. Brahmi script inscriptions recorded donor names, revealing ancient India's economic organization and religious patronage system.

Indigenous Engineering Features

Students observed sophisticated water management systems including rainwater harvesting channels, storage cisterns carved into rock, and overflow management. The guide explained rock-cutting techniques using iron chisels, hammers, heat-water methods, and sequential excavation requiring sophisticated project management.

Interactive Learning

Faculty facilitated group discussions, Q&A sessions with the guide, and documentation exercises. Five student groups focused on different aspects: architecture, engineering, history, art, and sustainability. A lunch break allowed informal discussions, followed by a final reflection session where students shared key takeaways.

Group 5 - Sustainability: 6 students (20%)

Each group was assigned a specific focus area to ensure comprehensive coverage of all aspects of the site. Groups later shared their findings with the entire class, creating a collaborative learning environment.

Lunch Break and Informal Learning

The group took a lunch break in the shaded area near the caves. This informal setting allowed for relaxed discussions where students shared their observations and insights. Faculty members used this opportunity to address any misconceptions and deepen understanding of the morning's learning.

Final Session and Reflection

Before departing, the entire group gathered for a final reflection session. Faculty members facilitated a structured discussion where students shared their key takeaways. Many students expressed how the visit transformed their understanding of ancient Indian knowledge systems from abstract concepts to tangible reality.

The guide concluded with remarks about the importance of heritage conservation and encouraged students to become ambassadors for preserving India's rich cultural legacy.

Return Journey

The return journey provided time for students to process their experiences and discuss their observations with peers and faculty. Many students expressed renewed appreciation for India's historical achievements and relevance of indigenous knowledge to contemporary challenges.
