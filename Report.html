Objective of the Activity: The primary objectives were to help students understand the management and preservation of historical and archaeological sites, particularly Buddhist rock-cut architecture from the 1st century BCE. Students were expected to gain insights into heritage tourism management and how such sites balance visitor access with conservation needs. The activity aimed to develop awareness about sustainable tourism practices and environmental challenges faced by heritage sites. By organizing a cleanliness campaign, it demonstrated corporate social responsibility and instilled civic duty and environmental stewardship. Additionally, the visit aimed to enhance understanding of stakeholder management involving government bodies, local communities, tourists, and conservation agencies. The activity sought to provide practical exposure to project management and team coordination through the cleanliness drive execution. Students were also expected to develop appreciation for India's rich cultural heritage and understand its potential as a sustainable tourism resource.

9. Details of the Activity: Our educational journey to Bedse Caves was an enriching experience that combined historical exploration with environmental consciousness. Students assembled at 9:30 A.M. for attendance and safety briefing. The group of 45 students with three faculty members departed at 10:00 A.M. The 45-minute journey through the Pune-Lonavala route provided valuable lessons about the Western Ghats region and its network of Buddhist cave complexes.

At the base of the hill, Dr. Jamnik provided a comprehensive orientation explaining that Bedse Caves are ancient Buddhist rock-cut monuments dating back to the 1st century BCE, belonging to the Hinayana phase of Buddhism. Located approximately 9 kilometers from Kamshet, these caves are less commercialized compared to the more famous Karla and Bhaja caves, making them an excellent location for studying sustainable heritage management practices.

The site consists of two main caves featuring intricate carvings, stupas, and prayer halls (chaityas) that showcase the remarkable craftsmanship of ancient Indian artisans. These caves were meticulously carved from a single basalt rock hill and served as monasteries for Buddhist monks. Their strategic location on ancient trade routes was discussed in detail, highlighting their historical importance as resting places for merchants and travelers traversing the region.

The guided exploration began at 11:00 A.M. with a 15-minute climb up the hill. This physical experience itself provided insights into the challenges of site accessibility and its impact on visitor numbers and overall site management. Along the way, students observed the rich biodiversity of the area and engaged in discussions about how environmental factors directly affect heritage conservation efforts.

Students were strategically divided into three groups, each spending 30-40 minutes examining different aspects of the caves. The first cave's magnificent chaitya hall, which houses a stupa at the rear, was particularly impressive. Students carefully examined the octagonal pillars, vaulted ceiling, and intricate facade carvings. Faculty members explained the sophisticated rock-cut architecture techniques, discussing the primitive tools, methods, and enormous amounts of time required for such construction, drawing insightful parallels to modern management principles including project planning, resource management, and quality control.

The second cave provided valuable insights into the daily monastic life of ancient Buddhist monks, featuring simple cells and ingeniously designed water cisterns. We discussed how the functional design reflected the Buddhist philosophy of minimalism and how modern organizational spaces can learn valuable lessons from such efficient space utilization. The panoramic view from the caves offered a deeper understanding of why this particular location was carefully chosen for meditation and spiritual practice.

Cleanliness Campaign - Practical Application of Corporate Social Responsibility

At 11:45 A.M., we initiated a comprehensive cleanliness campaign as a practical demonstration of corporate social responsibility and environmental stewardship. This hands-on activity transformed our visit from passive observation to active contribution. Students were divided into teams of 8-10 members, each equipped with gloves, garbage bags, and cleaning tools. Team leaders were designated to coordinate efforts within their assigned zones.

The campaign starkly revealed the extent of tourist negligence at heritage sites. We systematically collected substantial amounts of waste including plastic bottles, food wrappers, disposable cups, plastic bags, broken glass, and cigarette butts that were scattered throughout the site and surrounding areas. Over the course of 70 minutes, our dedicated teams collected approximately 28 kilograms of waste, filling more than 35 large garbage bags.

Proper waste segregation was conducted on-site, with teams carefully separating biodegradable and non-biodegradable materials. This practical exercise reinforced classroom lessons on waste management and environmental sustainability. All collected waste was responsibly transported and disposed of at designated municipal collection points, ensuring complete end-to-end responsibility for our environmental initiative.

Students meticulously documented the before-and-after conditions through photographs. The transformation was dramatic and deeply satisfying for all participants. Local villagers who witnessed our efforts expressed genuine appreciation, and some even joined in spontaneously, creating meaningful community engagement. One elderly villager mentioned that this was the first time he had seen students taking such proactive initiative at the site, which was particularly heartening to hear.

After the campaign concluded at 1:00 P.M., we took a zero-waste lunch break using reusable containers and water bottles, practicing what we had just preached. Following lunch, Dr. Jamnik organized a reflection session where students enthusiastically shared their experiences and learnings. The discussion covered various aspects including heritage conservation challenges, tourism management ethics, the role of businesses in community welfare, and individual responsibility toward cultural and natural resources.

During the session, students presented innovative ideas for improving site management including installation of better signage, strategically placed waste bins, visitor awareness campaigns, greater local community involvement in site maintenance, QR code-based information systems, and enhanced collaborations between educational institutions and archaeological departments. These discussions effectively connected theoretical management concepts with real-world applications.

The return journey commenced at 2:15 P.M., with students carrying not just memories but a deeper understanding of our role as future managers and responsible citizens. Despite physical exhaustion from the day's activities, there was an overwhelming sense of accomplishment and purpose. The group reached college by 2:45 P.M., concluding what proved to be a highly successful and transformative educational activity.

• Learning Outcome:

The visit provided multidimensional learning that extended far beyond textbook knowledge. From a heritage management perspective, students gained firsthand understanding of how historical sites require continuous maintenance, monitoring, and strategic planning to balance preservation needs with public access demands. Heritage conservation emerged not just as protecting physical structures but also as maintaining cultural and historical narratives for future generations.

In the realm of tourism management, we witnessed the practical challenges of managing heritage sites with limited infrastructure. The insufficient facilities such as parking areas, restrooms, waste bins, and information centers directly affect both visitor experience and site preservation efforts. Students understood that successful heritage tourism requires a holistic approach that carefully considers visitor needs, conservation requirements, local community interests, and environmental sustainability simultaneously.

The cleanliness campaign provided powerful learning about CSR and environmental management. The physical act of collecting waste created an emotional connection to environmental causes that classroom discussions rarely achieve. Students learned that effective CSR initiatives require meticulous planning, strong team coordination, proper resource allocation, and sustained commitment rather than one-time token gestures or publicity stunts.

The activity demonstrated essential leadership and teamwork skills in action. Team leaders practiced delegation, motivation, coordination, and real-time problem-solving under field conditions. As team members, students learned the critical importance of systematic approaches, maintaining clear communication channels, and working collaboratively toward common goals despite physical challenges and time constraints.

Students enhanced their project management capabilities significantly. From planning logistics and organizing transportation to executing the cleanliness campaign and ensuring proper waste disposal, they participated in the complete project lifecycle. The experience underscored the importance of thorough planning, efficient resource allocation, proactive risk assessment, continuous execution monitoring, and comprehensive post-project evaluation.

The visit fostered greater cultural awareness and appreciation for India's extraordinarily rich historical heritage. For many students, including those from the same region, this was their first visit to Bedse Caves. This realization sparked important thoughts about how many valuable heritage sites across India remain underutilized and underappreciated, representing untapped potential for cultural tourism and sustainable regional development.

The experience instilled a strong sense of civic responsibility and commitment to community service. Students realized that waiting passively for government authorities to address every environmental and social issue is neither practical nor sufficient. As educated individuals and future business leaders, they understood they possess both the capacity and the responsibility to contribute meaningfully to society through direct action and innovative solutions.

Finally, the activity fostered deep environmental consciousness and sustainable thinking. The cleanliness campaign made students critically aware of their own consumption patterns and waste generation habits. Many committed to adopting more sustainable practices in their daily lives including reducing single-use plastics, carrying reusable items consistently, being more mindful of their environmental footprint, and actively encouraging others to embrace similar practices. The entire experience proved transformative, successfully combining education with action, theory with practice, and individual learning with collective responsibility, demonstrating that management education must extend beyond classroom walls to understanding and addressing real-world challenges effectively.
